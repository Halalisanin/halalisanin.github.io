Before a recruiter in South Africa calls you for an interview, many of them will look you up. LinkedIn is the most common platform for this, but they may also check Facebook, look for your name in general search results, or verify information you provided on your CV.

This is not something to be anxious about. It is something to prepare for. Here is what to do.

## Start with LinkedIn — and take it seriously

LinkedIn is not just a digital CV. For South African professionals in HR, banking, administration, and customer service, it is increasingly the platform where opportunities are sourced, recruiter messages land, and professional credibility is built.

The basics that many people skip:

**A professional photo.** Not a selfie, not a graduation photo from ten years ago, not your Facebook profile picture. A clean, forward-facing photo where you look like you would on the first day of a professional job. LinkedIn profiles with photos receive far more engagement than those without.

**A headline that says more than your job title.** Instead of "Administrator at XYZ Company," try "HR & Office Administrator | Payroll Processing | Compliance | South Africa." Use the field to include the keywords recruiters search for.

**A summary section that sounds like a person.** Three to five sentences in your own voice about who you are, what you have done, and what you are looking for. This is not your CV. It is your professional introduction.

**Complete work history with descriptions.** The more information you provide, the more likely you are to appear in search results for specific skills and industries.

## Connect deliberately

You do not need thousands of connections. But you do need a network that reflects your professional world. Connect with:

- Former colleagues and managers
- Classmates who are working in your target industries
- Recruiters who specialise in your field (search "HR recruiter South Africa" or "banking recruiter Gauteng")
- People who post content you find useful — connecting with active people puts you in their network's view

When you connect with someone you do not know, send a brief note with your request. "Hi [name], I am an office administrator with experience in banking and HR. I would like to connect with professionals in this space." Personalised requests are accepted far more often than blank requests.

## Manage what Google finds

Search your own name. Look at the first two pages of results. If your social media profiles appear, check what is publicly visible. You do not have to delete your personal accounts — but make sure the public-facing version of your profiles would not concern a professional employer.

On Facebook specifically, review your privacy settings. Most people's settings allow far more public visibility than they realise.

## You do not need to post constantly

There is a myth that building a professional online presence requires publishing thought-leadership articles every week. It does not — especially at the early stages of your career.

What is effective:
- Keeping your LinkedIn profile complete and up to date
- Liking and commenting thoughtfully on posts from people in your industry
- Sharing job-seeking or career resources that others in your network might find useful (like posts from this blog)
- Occasionally posting about a professional milestone or a course you completed

Consistent, low-effort engagement builds presence over time. It does not require daily effort.

## The simplest rule

Your online presence should pass one test: if a recruiter looked at everything public-facing about you today, would they see a professional, capable person who takes their career seriously?

If the answer is yes, you are in good shape. If not, now is the time to fix it.
