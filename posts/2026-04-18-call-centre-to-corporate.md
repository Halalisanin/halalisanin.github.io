There is a narrative in South Africa that the call centre is where careers go to stall. That it is something you do while you wait for a "real job." I want to challenge that directly, because I have seen what happens when people treat their call centre role as a training ground rather than a holding pattern.

The skills you build in a high-volume contact centre — composure, clarity, speed, and the ability to handle difficult people professionally — are exactly what corporate South Africa looks for in its best administrators, team leaders, and client-facing managers.

The difference between people who stay in the call centre and people who move out of it is not talent. It is intention.

## Treat every call as a rehearsal for a bigger room

When you are handling 60 calls a day with a two-minute average handle time, you are doing something that most people with university degrees have never done: communicating under real pressure with real consequences. You are learning to listen quickly, diagnose problems accurately, and provide clear answers without losing control of the conversation.

That is a skill. Name it. Track it. When you are asked in a future interview "how do you work under pressure?" you will have 12 months of evidence to draw from.

## Get something down in writing

Ask your team leader or manager for written feedback — ideally quarterly, even if it is informal. Get your performance stats: calls handled, quality scores, FCR (first call resolution) rates. These become bullet points on your CV. "Maintained 94% quality score across 8,000 monthly calls" is a specific, verifiable achievement that most office admin candidates cannot match.

## Signal that you want to grow

In most South African call centres, the people who get promoted are not always the top performers — they are the people their supervisors know want to move up. Be explicit. Tell your team leader, your coach, your manager: "I want to move into a training role / team lead position / back-office function. What would I need to demonstrate?"

Then do exactly that.

## Build skills on the side

The hours in a call centre are often shift-based, which means you may have regular blocks of free time at unusual hours. Use them. Free or low-cost options available to South Africans right now:

- **Microsoft Office training** — Word, Excel, PowerPoint via YouTube or government digital hubs
- **SETA-funded short courses** — SABPP, MICT SETA, and Services SETA all offer funded skills programmes
- **Online certifications** — Google Career Certificates are free and credible in data, digital marketing, and project management

Every certificate you add to your CV signals that you are investing in yourself. That is exactly what employers want to see.

## Make the lateral move

Sometimes the fastest route up is sideways first. A move from a call centre agent role to a back-office processing clerk role at the same company — even at a similar salary — gives you:

- A different job title on your CV
- Exposure to administrative systems and processes
- An internal reference from a different department
- A clearer path toward an office admin career

Do not be too proud for the sideways move. Five years from now, that step across will look like a step up.
