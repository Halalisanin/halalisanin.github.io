## Why Most CV Bullets Fail

Open almost any CV and you'll find bullet points like these:

- *Responsible for answering customer queries*
- *Assisted with filing and administration*
- *Performed data capturing duties*

These tell a recruiter absolutely nothing useful. Every candidate applying for the same role will have similar responsibilities. What the recruiter needs to know is: **how well did you do those things, and what was the result?**

Duty-based bullets describe what the role was. Achievement-based bullets describe what *you* brought to the role. That's the difference between getting shortlisted and getting ignored.

## The Formula for a Strong Bullet Point

The most effective CV bullets follow this structure:

**[Action Verb] + [What You Did] + [Result or Scale]**

For example:
- *Resolved an average of 85 customer complaints per day, maintaining a first-call resolution rate of 91%*
- *Reduced document processing time by 30% by redesigning the internal filing system*
- *Trained and mentored five new team members on CRM and compliance procedures*

You don't need a number in every bullet, but you do need specificity. *"Handled customer complaints"* is weak. *"Resolved high-volume inbound complaints for a team of 12 consultants"* is far stronger.

## Start Every Bullet With a Strong Action Verb

Weak bullets start with "responsible for," "assisted with," or "helped with." These are passive and vague.

Strong bullets start with action verbs that show initiative and impact:

**For customer service and contact centre roles:**
- Resolved, Responded, De-escalated, Handled, Managed, Guided, Processed, Coordinated

**For administrative and office support roles:**
- Organised, Maintained, Processed, Drafted, Compiled, Scheduled, Coordinated, Implemented

**For banking and finance roles:**
- Reconciled, Audited, Processed, Verified, Analysed, Reported, Managed, Administered

**For team leadership and management:**
- Led, Supervised, Trained, Mentored, Directed, Coordinated, Motivated, Developed

## How Many Bullets Per Role?

- **Most recent / most relevant role:** 5-7 bullets
- **Previous roles:** 3-4 bullets
- **Older or less relevant roles:** 1-2 bullets, or none at all

Quality over quantity. Five strong, specific bullets beat ten vague ones every time.

## Examples Across Common South African Roles

### Customer Service / Call Centre
**Weak:** Answered inbound calls from customers
**Strong:** Managed 90+ inbound calls daily for a national insurance provider, resolving queries within SLA targets and achieving a customer satisfaction rating of 4.7/5

### Office Administrator
**Weak:** Responsible for general office duties
**Strong:** Coordinated diary management, travel bookings, and minute-taking for a team of eight, reducing scheduling conflicts by 40%

### Data Capturer
**Weak:** Captured data on the system
**Strong:** Processed and verified 500+ records daily with a 99.8% accuracy rate, meeting all compliance requirements under POPIA

### Human Resources Administrator
**Weak:** Helped with HR admin
**Strong:** Administered end-to-end recruitment for 15-20 vacancies simultaneously, coordinating interviews, reference checks, and onboarding documentation

### Banking / Teller
**Weak:** Assisted customers with banking transactions
**Strong:** Processed an average of 120 teller transactions per day while maintaining a zero cash discrepancy record over 18 months

## What NOT to Include in Your CV Bullets

- **Vague adjectives:** *"hardworking," "dedicated," "passionate"*, show it, don't say it
- **Obvious duties:** If everyone in that role does something, listing it adds no value unless you can quantify or contextualise it
- **Personal pronouns:** Don't write *"I managed the team"*, write *"Managed the team"*
- **Irrelevant responsibilities:** If you're applying for a customer service role, your bullet about ordering stationery isn't relevant, cut it
- **Full sentences with unnecessary words:** *"My main responsibility was to..."*, cut to the verb

## The Skills Section vs the Experience Section

Many candidates confuse these two. Your **skills section** is a short list, software, languages, certifications. Your **experience section** with bullet points is where you demonstrate *how* you used those skills.

Don't use your experience bullets to restate what's already in your skills section. *"Proficient in Microsoft Excel"* belongs in skills. *"Built monthly reporting dashboards in Excel that reduced finance team reporting time from two days to four hours"* belongs in experience.

## A Final Check

Before you submit your CV, go through each bullet point and ask:

1. Does this start with an action verb?
2. Does it say more than just what my job description said?
3. Can I add a number, scale, or result to make it more specific?
4. Is it relevant to the role I'm applying for?

If you can answer yes to all four, your bullets are doing their job.
