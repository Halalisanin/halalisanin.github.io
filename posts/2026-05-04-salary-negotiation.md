## Why You Should Always Negotiate

The single most consistent finding in salary research is this: people who negotiate earn more than people who do not. Over a career, the difference is substantial — sometimes hundreds of thousands of rands.

Most employers expect negotiation. When they make an offer, they often have room to move — and they know it. The first offer is rarely the best offer.

The fear of negotiating — that you will seem greedy, damage the relationship, or lose the offer — is almost never realised. Recruiters and hiring managers respect candidates who know their worth and can advocate for themselves professionally. It is the way you negotiate that matters, not whether you do.

## Before You Negotiate: Know Your Numbers

Walking into a salary conversation without knowing your market rate is like going to buy a car without knowing what the model is worth. You need information.

**Where to research South African salary benchmarks:**

- **PayScale South Africa** (payscale.com) — role-specific data
- **Careers24 Salary Guide** — industry overviews
- **LinkedIn Salary Insights** — available for some roles
- **Recruitment agencies** — consultants at agencies like Adcorp, Kelly, or Isilumko can give you current market intelligence for your sector
- **Conversations with peers** — awkward but informative

Know the range for your:
- Job title and level
- Industry (banking pays differently to NGO sector)
- Location (Johannesburg and Cape Town typically pay more than other cities)
- Years of experience

Come to the conversation with a specific, researched number — not a vague sense that you "want more."

## Timing: When to Raise the Salary Question

**Do not raise salary in the first or second interview** unless the employer asks. Lead with your value and interest in the role. Bringing up money too early signals that salary is your primary motivation.

The right moment is when you have received a formal offer. At that point, you have leverage: they have chosen you. They do not want to restart the process.

If asked about salary expectations early in the process, it is reasonable to say:
> *"I am flexible and more interested in the right fit at this stage. I would prefer to discuss specifics once we've both established that there's a strong match."*

Or give a range based on your research:
> *"Based on my experience and the market rate for this type of role, I would expect something in the range of R18,000 to R22,000 per month — but I am open to discussing the full package."*

## How to Respond to the Initial Offer

When an offer comes in, do not feel pressured to respond immediately. It is entirely professional to say:

> *"Thank you so much — I am genuinely excited about this opportunity. Would you be able to give me 24 to 48 hours to review the full offer details?"*

This gives you time to:
- Consider the full package (not just the base salary)
- Compare it to your research
- Decide what you want to ask for

## The Negotiation Conversation

When you are ready to negotiate, be direct, specific, and warm. You are not making demands — you are having a professional conversation.

**Script:**
> *"Thank you for the offer — I am very enthusiastic about joining the team. Based on my [X years of experience / specific skills / market research], I was hoping we could discuss a base salary of R[your number]. Is there flexibility there?"*

Then be quiet. Let them respond.

**Key principles:**
- **Name a specific number, not a range.** A range signals that you are open to the lower end. If you say "between R20,000 and R25,000," you will get R20,000.
- **Anchor high (but realistically).** Ask for slightly more than your ideal so that when you meet in the middle, you end up where you want to be.
- **Use market data as your justification**, not personal need. "I have a bond to pay" is not a negotiation argument. "The market rate for this role in Johannesburg is R22,000–R26,000" is.
- **Be friendly, not adversarial.** You are going to work with these people. Keep the tone collaborative.

## What to Do If They Cannot Move on Base Salary

Many employers — especially in the public sector, large corporates with rigid salary bands, and NGOs — have limited flexibility on base salary. If the answer is no, do not walk away without exploring the full package.

**Other things you can negotiate:**
- Annual leave days (can you go from 15 to 18 days?)
- Remote work or flexible hours
- Start date (sometimes useful if you need time to wrap up your current role)
- Performance review timeline (can the first review be at six months instead of a year?)
- Professional development budget (courses, certifications)
- Transport or travel allowance
- Medical aid contribution

The total value of your package is what matters, not just the number on the salary slip.

## When to Accept the First Offer

There are situations where negotiating is not appropriate or wise:

- The offer already exceeds your market value or your expectations
- You are entering a highly competitive role where many candidates were considered
- The organisation is a non-profit or start-up with transparent, fixed salary scales
- You negotiated extensively during the process and this is their final position

Negotiating when you genuinely have no leverage, or when you have already been told the salary is fixed, creates unnecessary friction. Read the room.

## The Outcome

Whether you negotiate successfully or not, accept the outcome graciously. If they move on salary, express genuine gratitude. If they cannot, acknowledge it professionally and decide whether the role is still right for you.

A salary negotiation is not a confrontation. It is two professionals trying to reach an agreement that works for both parties. Handled well, it sets a tone of confidence and respect that will serve you throughout your tenure.

Know your worth. Ask for it. Then deliver it.
