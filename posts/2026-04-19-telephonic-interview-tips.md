Telephonic screening interviews are the first real filter in South African hiring for most call centre, admin, banking, and HR roles. The recruiter or HR officer is assessing you in the first 60 seconds — and they are doing it entirely through how you sound, what you say, and how quickly you say it.

Most candidates fail telephonic interviews for reasons that have nothing to do with their qualifications. Here is how to not be one of them.

## Treat it like a face-to-face interview

The single biggest mistake candidates make is treating a phone screen casually because "it is only a call." It is not only a call. It is the interview that decides whether you get to the interview.

Prepare the same way you would for an in-person meeting:
- Know the company and the role
- Have your CV in front of you
- Have notes on your key talking points
- Be dressed — it affects your posture and your tone, even on a call

## Control your environment completely

Find a quiet space. This is non-negotiable. Background noise — a TV, a baby, traffic, a neighbour — signals to the recruiter that you did not take this seriously. If you live in a busy household, go outside, go to a library, go somewhere quiet.

Also: charge your phone the night before. Running out of battery during a telephonic interview is a disqualifier.

If you know the call is coming on a specific line, test the signal at that location beforehand.

## Speak clearly and at a controlled pace

Nerves make people speak faster. Faster speech is harder to follow on a phone call, especially across South Africa's diverse accents and sometimes inconsistent network quality. Slow down by about 20% from your normal pace. Pause between points. Let the interviewer finish their question before you start your answer.

Do not be afraid of two seconds of silence while you collect your thoughts. That is normal. Filling silence with filler words ("um," "like," "you know") is what undermines you.

## Have your answer to "tell me about yourself" ready

This question opens almost every telephonic screen. You have about 90 seconds. Cover:
1. Who you are professionally (current or most recent role)
2. What you have done (two or three key experiences)
3. Why you are interested in this specific opportunity

Do not give your full life story. Do not recite your CV chronologically. Give them a confident, specific summary that makes them want to ask more.

## Ask one good question at the end

When they ask "do you have any questions for us?" — and they will — have one ready. Good options:
- "What does the onboarding process look like for this role?"
- "What would success in the first 90 days look like?"
- "What is the team structure I would be joining?"

Saying "no, I think you covered everything" is a missed opportunity. It also signals low initiative.

## Follow up

If you have an email address for the recruiter, send a brief thank-you email within 24 hours. It takes five minutes and very few candidates do it. It keeps your name in front of them and signals professionalism.
