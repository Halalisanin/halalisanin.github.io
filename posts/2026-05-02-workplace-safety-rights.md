## Your Rights Under OHSA

The **Occupational Health and Safety Act 85 of 1993 (OHSA)** doesn't only place duties on employers. It explicitly grants rights to workers, rights that are enforceable and that employers can't legitimately override.

Understanding these rights is the first step to using them.

## The Right to a Safe Working Environment

Your employer is legally required to provide a working environment that's, as far as reasonably practicable, safe and without risk to your health. This isn't aspirational language, it's a legal obligation with criminal penalties for non-compliance.

"Reasonably practicable" means that employers can't simply claim something is too expensive or inconvenient to fix. They must weigh the cost of safety measures against the seriousness of the risk. For serious hazards, significant expense is legally justified.

If your workplace is unsafe, exposed wiring, broken equipment, inadequate ventilation, chemical exposure without protection, structural hazards, this is a violation of OHSA, and you've the right to report it.

## The Right to Refuse Unsafe Work

This is the right most workers don't know about, and it's one of the most important.

Under Section 27 of OHSA, an employee may **refuse to perform work that poses a risk of serious injury or death**, provided the risk isn't an inherent part of the job (e.g., a firefighter can't refuse to enter a burning building as a matter of course, but they can refuse if their protective equipment is defective).

**To invoke this right correctly:**
1. Inform your employer or supervisor of the specific danger
2. State that you're unable to perform the task safely under current conditions
3. This should be done in writing if at all possible, so there's a record

Critically, **an employer can't discipline, dismiss, or intimidate an employee for legitimately refusing unsafe work.** Doing so is itself a violation of OHSA and of the Labour Relations Act, and can give rise to an unfair dismissal claim.

## The Right to a Health and Safety Representative

In any workplace with 20 or more employees, workers are entitled to **elect a health and safety representative** from among themselves. This person has specific rights:

- To inspect the workplace at least once every month
- To investigate incidents and near misses
- To review health and safety reports
- To accompany an inspector from the Department of Employment and Labour during inspections
- To make recommendations to the employer on safety matters

The employer is **required to consult** with health and safety representatives. This isn't optional.

If your workplace has 20 or more employees and no health and safety representative has been elected, this is a violation of OHSA. You can raise this with your employer or report it to the Department.

## The Right to Information About Hazards

Employers must inform workers about any hazard in the workplace that affects their health and safety, in a language and manner they can understand. You've the right to know:

- What hazardous substances you're exposed to
- What the health effects of those substances are
- What protective measures are in place
- What to do in an emergency

You also have the right to see relevant safety data sheets and reports. This is particularly important in manufacturing, mining (where applicable), construction, cleaning, and healthcare environments.

## The Right to Report Violations Without Retaliation

Employees who report health and safety violations to the Department of Employment and Labour are protected against victimisation under OHSA and the Protected Disclosures Act.

If your employer retaliates against you for reporting a safety violation, by dismissing you, demoting you, reducing your pay, or creating a hostile environment, this is unlawful. You can pursue this through the CCMA or the Labour Court.

## How to Report a Health and Safety Violation

**Step 1: Report internally first.** Raise the concern with your health and safety representative or directly with management, in writing. Keep a copy.

**Step 2: If the employer fails to act**, contact the **Department of Employment and Labour**. An inspector will be assigned to investigate.

- **Call:** 0860 105 350
- **Visit:** Any Department of Employment and Labour office
- **Online:** www.labour.gov.za

Inspectors have broad powers to enter the workplace without notice and issue compliance and prohibition notices. A prohibition notice stops work in the affected area immediately until the violation is corrected.

**Step 3: Contact your trade union** if you're a member. Unions often have dedicated health and safety officers who can assist.

## Protecting Yourself Day to Day

Knowing your rights is powerful. So is developing a safety-conscious mindset:

- Report near misses and minor hazards before they become serious incidents
- Never assume someone else has reported a hazard
- Use the PPE provided, every time
- Attend all health and safety training your employer provides
- Know where the first aid kit, fire extinguishers, and emergency exits are

Your employer has legal obligations. You've legal rights. A safe workplace is built when both sides take their responsibilities seriously, and when workers aren't afraid to speak up.
