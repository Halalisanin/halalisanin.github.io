Most South Africans know that the Labour Relations Act protects employees. Fewer know that job seekers, people who haven't yet been hired, also have rights under South African law. Knowing these rights protects you from exploitation and helps you spot when something is wrong with a recruitment process.

## You can't be discriminated against unfairly

The Employment Equity Act prohibits discrimination during recruitment on the basis of race, gender, sex, pregnancy, marital status, family responsibility, ethnic or social origin, colour, sexual orientation, age, disability, religion, HIV status, conscience, belief, political opinion, culture, language, or birth.

This applies to job advertisements, shortlisting decisions, interview questions, and offers. An employer can't legally ask you in an interview how many children you've, whether you're pregnant, or what your HIV status is.

If you believe you were discriminated against in a hiring process, you can refer a dispute to the CCMA (Commission for Conciliation, Mediation and Arbitration) or the Equality Court.

## You don't have to pay to apply for a job

Legitimate recruitment agencies and employers don't charge job seekers fees for applications, placements, or training as a condition of getting a job. If someone asks you to pay money to be considered for a role, it's almost certainly a scam.

This is regulated by the Private Security Industry Regulatory Authority and broader consumer protection frameworks. Report suspected scams to the Department of Employment and Labour.

## Your personal information is protected

The Protection of Personal Information Act (POPIA) applies to recruitment. Employers and agencies that collect your personal information, your ID number, address, banking details, references, are required to:

- Only collect what they actually need
- Store it securely
- Not share it with third parties without your consent
- Delete it if you request it and there's no lawful reason to retain it

Be cautious about how much personal information you share early in a recruitment process. Legitimate employers don't need your ID number at the application stage.

## You're entitled to honest feedback (even if they won't always give it)

South African law doesn't require employers to provide reasons for rejection, but the Employment Equity Act requires that selection processes be fair and justifiable. If you suspect a rejection was discriminatory, you've recourse.

On a practical level: if you're rejected after an interview, it's completely acceptable to ask for feedback. Many employers won't respond. Some will. The ones who do give you information you can use.

## Probation has rules

If you're offered a job and it includes a probationary period, your employer can't simply let you go at the end of that period without following a fair process. The LRA requires that probationary dismissals include:

- Assessment of performance against communicated standards
- An opportunity for the employee to respond
- A proper process, not just "we decided not to keep you"

Know this before you sign a contract. Read the probation clauses carefully.

## Where to get help

- **CCMA:** 0861 16 16 16, for unfair labour practices and disputes
- **Department of Employment and Labour:** 0800 843 843, for general employment queries
- **Legal Aid South Africa:** 0800 110 110, for free legal assistance if you qualify

Your rights exist whether or not you know about them. But knowing about them means you can use them.
