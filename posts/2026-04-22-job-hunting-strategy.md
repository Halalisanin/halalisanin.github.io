## Why Most Job Searches Fail

The average South African job seeker sends out dozens of applications, refreshes their email obsessively, and then wonders why nothing is happening. The issue is almost never a lack of effort. It's a lack of strategy.

Spraying applications across every platform hoping something sticks is exhausting and demoralising. A targeted, structured approach will get you further in two weeks than six months of random applying.

## Know Exactly What You Are Targeting

Before you write a single application, get specific. Ask yourself:

- What industry do I want to work in?
- What size company suits me (corporate, SME, government)?
- What location am I willing to work in, or am I open to remote?
- What salary range do I need, not just want?

Vague job seekers get vague results. The more precise your target, the sharper your applications become.

## Use the Right Platforms for South Africa

Not all job boards are equal. Here's where to focus your energy:

- **PNet**, still the dominant platform for corporate, admin, and banking roles
- **LinkedIn**, essential for professional visibility and direct outreach to recruiters
- **CareerJunction**, strong for mid-level professional roles
- **Indeed SA**, good volume, especially for entry-level and operations roles
- **DPSA** (dpsa.gov.za), for all government and public sector posts
- **Company career pages**, apply directly on employer websites; many roles are never posted publicly

Set up job alerts on each platform with specific keywords. Don't browse aimlessly, let alerts bring relevant positions to you.

## Tailor Every Application

The biggest mistake job seekers make is sending the same generic CV and cover letter to every position. Recruiters can tell immediately.

For each role you apply to:

1. Read the job description carefully, twice
2. Identify the three or four skills they emphasise most
3. Make sure your CV explicitly reflects those skills with real examples
4. Write a cover letter that references the company by name and speaks to that specific role

This takes more time per application, but your response rate will increase significantly.

## The Hidden Job Market

Studies consistently show that 60-70% of jobs are never advertised. They're filled through referrals, internal promotions, and recruiters tapping their networks.

This means your network is your most powerful job hunting tool. Steps to activate it:

- Update your LinkedIn profile and set it to "Open to Work" (visible to recruiters only)
- Contact former colleagues, managers, and classmates, not to beg for a job, but to let them know you're looking
- Attend industry events, webinars, and professional groups
- Engage with companies you want to work for on LinkedIn, comment, share, connect with employees

One genuine conversation can unlock an opportunity that no job board ever would.

## Keep Track of Your Applications

As soon as your search grows beyond a handful of applications, things blur together. You forget what you applied for, when, and what happened.

Use a simple spreadsheet with columns for:

- Company name
- Role title
- Date applied
- Platform used
- Status (applied / interview / rejected / offer)
- Notes

This keeps you organised, helps you spot patterns, and prevents the embarrassment of applying for the same role twice.

## Following Up Without Being Annoying

If you haven't heard back within two weeks of applying, a short, professional follow-up email is entirely appropriate. Keep it brief:

> *"Good day, I submitted my application for the [Role] position on [Date] and wanted to confirm it was received. I remain very interested in the opportunity and would welcome the chance to discuss my experience further."*

Don't follow up more than once per application. Persistence is appreciated; pestering isn't.

## Managing the Emotional Side of Job Hunting

Rejection is part of the process, for everyone, at every level. A job search in South Africa can take three to six months even for strong candidates, especially in a competitive market.

Protect your energy by:

- Setting daily limits on how long you spend job hunting (two to three focused hours beats an exhausting eight-hour scroll)
- Celebrating small wins, an interview invitation, a recruiter call, even a useful connection
- Staying in a routine that maintains your confidence and mental health
- Being honest with your support network about where you're

The job search is temporary. Your professionalism and reputation are permanent. Handle every interaction, rejections included, with grace.

## The Bottom Line

A smart job search is focused, structured, and relational. Know your target, use the right platforms, tailor your applications, and invest in your network. Track everything. Follow up thoughtfully. And be patient with yourself, this takes time, but the right role is out there.
