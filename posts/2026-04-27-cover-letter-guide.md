## Why the Cover Letter Still Matters

In an age of online applications and automated screening, you might wonder whether cover letters are still read. The answer is: the good ones are.

A weak, generic cover letter will be skipped. But a targeted, confident, well-written letter can be the thing that gets you into the interview pile when two candidates have nearly identical CVs. It's your first chance to speak in your own voice, before you ever set foot in an interview.

## The Purpose of a Cover Letter

A cover letter isn't a summary of your CV. That's a very common mistake. The recruiter has your CV right there, they don't need you to repeat it.

A cover letter does three things:
1. Explains **why you want this specific role at this specific organisation**
2. Highlights **the one or two things about your background that make you the right fit** for this particular position
3. Demonstrates **that you can communicate professionally and concisely**

## The Structure

Keep it to **one page**. Three to four short, focused paragraphs.

### Opening Paragraph. Hook Them Immediately

Don't open with "I'm writing to apply for the position of...", recruiters read this ten times a day and their eyes glaze over.

Instead, open with something that shows you've done your homework or speaks to your genuine motivation. For example:

> *"Absa's focus on accessible banking for underserved communities is precisely what drew me to this Customer Service Consultant role. After three years supporting clients through complex financial queries at FNB, I'm ready to bring that experience to a bank whose values align more closely with my own."*

This immediately shows: interest in the organisation, relevant experience, and professional confidence.

### Body Paragraph. Make Your Case

In one to two short paragraphs, connect your most relevant experience directly to the requirements in the job description.

Pick the two or three skills or experiences the employer has emphasised most, and give a concrete example of how you've demonstrated each one. Use the language from the job description. Don't list everything you've done; choose the most relevant.

> *"In my current role, I handle an average of 80 inbound calls per day, consistently achieving a first-call resolution rate above 92%. I'm experienced with CRM systems, including Salesforce, and I've a strong record of de-escalating difficult customer situations, a skill I understand is central to this role."*

### Closing Paragraph. Confident and Clear

Close by expressing enthusiasm for the opportunity and stating clearly what you would like to happen next.

> *"I'm excited about the opportunity to contribute to your team and would welcome the chance to discuss my application further. I'm available for an interview at your convenience and can provide references upon request. Thank you for your time and consideration."*

Sign off professionally: *Yours sincerely* (if you addressed someone by name) or *Yours faithfully* (if you used "Dear Sir/Madam").

## Cover Letter Mistakes to Avoid

**Writing the same letter for every application.** Recruiters can tell. A letter with no company name, no specific role details, and generic phrases reads as lazy and uninterested.

**Focusing entirely on yourself.** Instead of "I want this job because it will help me grow," try "I'm drawn to this role because my experience in X directly supports what your team is trying to achieve."

**Being too long.** One page. Four paragraphs maximum. Brevity signals respect for the reader's time.

**Repeating your CV verbatim.** Summarise, don't copy. The cover letter should add context and personality, not just bullet points in paragraph form.

**Spelling and grammar errors.** Read it aloud before sending. Use spell check. Then ask someone else to read it. Errors signal carelessness and are nearly impossible to recover from.

**Attaching the wrong letter.** With many tailored letters in circulation, it's easy to send the Nedbank letter to Standard Bank. Double-check every time.

## Formatting Tips for South Africa

- Font: Arial, Calibri, or Times New Roman, size 11 or 12
- Use your contact details at the top (name, phone, email, location)
- Include the date and the recruiter's name if you've it
- Address it to a specific person where possible ,  "Dear Ms Mokoena" is always better than "Dear Hiring Manager"
- Attach as a PDF unless specifically asked for Word format

## A Note on Length and Tone

South African workplaces tend to value professionalism, respect, and directness. Your cover letter should be formal but not stiff, confident but not arrogant. Write in complete sentences. Avoid abbreviations and slang.

You're presenting your professional self, make sure the letter reflects the best version of who you're.
