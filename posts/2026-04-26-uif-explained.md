## What Is the UIF?

The **Unemployment Insurance Fund (UIF)** is administered by the Department of Employment and Labour under the **Unemployment Insurance Act 63 of 2001** and the **Unemployment Insurance Contributions Act 4 of 2002**.

Its purpose is to provide short-term financial relief to workers who lose income because they:

- Are retrenched or dismissed
- Are on maternity leave
- Are on adoption leave (or commissioning parent leave)
- Are unable to work due to illness
- Have a dependent who was a contributing worker and has died

The UIF isn't welfare, it's a contributory insurance scheme that workers pay into throughout their employment. When you need it, you're claiming money that you and your employer have specifically contributed towards.

## Who Contributes to the UIF?

Every employee who works more than 24 hours per month for an employer must contribute to the UIF. Both the employee and the employer contribute:

- **Employee contribution:** 1% of monthly earnings
- **Employer contribution:** 1% of the employee's monthly earnings
- **Total:** 2% per month, of which the employer remits both portions to SARS

**Who's excluded from contributing (and therefore can't claim):**
- Workers working fewer than 24 hours per month
- Public servants employed by the national or provincial government (they've a separate scheme)
- Workers who receive a monthly remuneration above the earnings ceiling (currently approximately R17,712 per month, check with the Department for the current figure)
- Non-South African citizens on work permits (with some exceptions)

## What Benefits Can You Claim?

### 1. Unemployment Benefits
If you're retrenched, dismissed, or your contract expires and you can't find work, you can claim unemployment benefits.

You receive **38% of your average daily earnings**, calculated over the four years of contributions preceding your claim, up to a maximum percentage cap. The benefit scales slightly upward for lower-income earners.

You can claim for a maximum of **365 days** over a four-year period (credits accumulate at one day of credit for every four days worked, up to 365 days total).

### 2. Illness Benefits
If you're unable to work for more than 14 days due to illness or injury (that's NOT work-related, for work injuries see the Compensation Fund), you may claim illness benefits.

Benefits are paid at the same rate as unemployment benefits (approximately 38-58% of earnings). You can claim for up to 365 days (shared across all benefit types) and must provide a medical certificate.

### 3. Maternity Benefits
Pregnant employees can claim maternity benefits for up to **17.32 weeks** (approximately four months). This is separate from maternity leave under the BCEA. UIF pays a benefit during the leave period.

Benefits are paid at 66% of daily earnings (capped at the maximum).

This is significantly underutilised. Many South African women don't know they're entitled to UIF maternity benefits in addition to whatever their employer may or may not pay during leave.

### 4. Adoption and Commissioning Parent Benefits
Employees who adopt a child under two years of age, or who are commissioning parents in a surrogate motherhood arrangement, can claim benefits for up to 10 weeks.

### 5. Dependant's Benefits
If a contributing worker dies, their surviving spouse, life partner, or dependent children can claim dependant's benefits, up to the full 365 days of credits the deceased worker had accumulated.

## How to Claim UIF

### Option 1: uFiling Online (Recommended)
The quickest way to claim is through **uFiling** at uFiling.labour.gov.za.

You'll need:
- South African ID number
- Contact details
- Banking details
- Employment history (the Department will verify contributions)

### Option 2: Visit a Labour Centre
Visit your nearest Department of Employment and Labour labour centre with the following documents:
- Original South African ID or certified copy
- UI19 form (completed by your former employer, request this when you leave)
- Last six payslips
- Bank statement (not older than three months)
- If retrenched: a letter of retrenchment or dismissal

### Important: Register Within Six Months
You must register your claim **within six months** of losing employment. After six months, you lose the credits accumulated during that employment. Don't delay.

## Common Problems and How to Handle Them

**Problem: My employer never registered me with UIF.**
Your employer is legally obligated to register you and contribute. If they haven't, report them to the Department of Employment and Labour. You may still be able to claim, and your employer will face penalties.

**Problem: My employer won't give me a UI19 form.**
This form is a legal requirement. If your employer refuses, you can still submit a claim and the Department will contact the employer directly.

**Problem: My claim has been pending for months.**
Unfortunately, UIF processing can be slow. Follow up at your labour centre, keep all reference numbers, and escalate to the provincial office if necessary.

## The Bottom Line

UIF contributions are deducted from your salary every month. That money belongs to you, it's your safety net. Claim it when you need it. Don't let pride, ignorance of the process, or an unhelpful employer prevent you from accessing a benefit you've earned.

For further assistance: **Call the UIF call centre at 0800 843 843** (toll-free).
