## What Is Injury on Duty?

An injury on duty (IOD) is any injury, illness, or death that occurs as a result of, or in the course of, an employee's employment. This is governed by the **Compensation for Occupational Injuries and Diseases Act 130 of 1993 (COIDA)**.

Contrary to what many workers believe, IOD covers a wide range of situations, not just accidents on a factory floor. It includes:

- Physical injuries that occur at the workplace
- Injuries sustained while travelling on behalf of the employer (but not commuting to and from work)
- Occupational diseases caused by conditions at work (such as hearing loss from noise, or respiratory conditions from chemical exposure)
- Mental health conditions arising directly from the employment environment in some circumstances

COIDA provides a no-fault compensation system, meaning you don't need to prove your employer was negligent to receive compensation. You only need to show the injury occurred in the course of employment.

## What Is NOT Covered

It's equally important to understand what COIDA doesn't cover:

- Injuries sustained while commuting to or from work (unless the employer provided the transport)
- Injuries caused by the employee's own serious and wilful misconduct (unless the injury results in death or permanent disablement)
- Domestic workers employed in private households (though there are separate provisions under certain circumstances)

## Step 1: Report the Injury Immediately

The moment an injury occurs, you must inform your employer. This isn't optional, delayed reporting can complicate or even disqualify your claim.

Your employer is legally required to provide you with a **WCL2 form** (First Medical Report from Employee). This form must be completed and submitted to the Compensation Commissioner.

Don't assume your employer will handle everything automatically. Ask directly:
*"Has my injury been reported to the Compensation Fund?"*

## Step 2: Seek Medical Treatment

Go to a doctor or hospital immediately. Inform the medical practitioner that your injury is work-related. They'll complete a **WCL1 form** (First Medical Report from Medical Practitioner) and submit it to the Compensation Fund.

You're entitled to have your medical costs covered by the Compensation Fund, not by your medical aid. Your employer shouldn't deduct from your sick leave for a legitimate IOD, this is a common abuse that employees should know to push back on.

## Step 3: Your Employer's Obligations

Your employer is legally required to:

1. Report the accident to the Compensation Commissioner within **seven days** of the accident (or within 14 days if it results in death)
2. Complete the **WCL2 form** and submit it
3. Pay you your full salary for the **first three months** if you're unable to work due to the injury (to be recovered from the Compensation Fund)
4. Not dismiss you solely because of an injury on duty

Failure to report an IOD is a criminal offence under COIDA.

## What Compensation Can You Claim?

COIDA provides several categories of compensation:

- **Temporary Total Disablement (TTD):** If you can't work due to the injury, you receive 75% of your earnings for up to 24 months
- **Permanent Disablement:** If the injury results in permanent disability, compensation is calculated based on the degree of disablement and your earnings. Total permanent disablement results in a pension for life
- **Medical expenses:** All reasonable and necessary medical treatment, including rehabilitation
- **Death benefits:** If an employee dies due to an occupational injury, dependants receive a funeral allowance and a pension

## If Your Employer Refuses to Help

Unfortunately, some employers try to discourage employees from reporting injuries or downplay the severity. If this happens:

1. Put your report in writing (email or letter) so there's a record
2. Contact the **Department of Employment and Labour** directly at 0860 105 350
3. You can submit a claim directly to the Compensation Fund without employer cooperation, though this is slower
4. Consult a trade union representative or labour lawyer if necessary

## Common Mistakes Workers Make

- **Waiting too long to report:** The longer you wait, the harder it becomes to prove the injury was work-related
- **Not going to a doctor:** Verbal reports without medical documentation are very difficult to act on
- **Using personal medical aid:** You're entitled to COIDA coverage; using your own medical aid wastes your benefits and complicates the claim
- **Assuming the employer has handled it:** Always confirm with a reference number
