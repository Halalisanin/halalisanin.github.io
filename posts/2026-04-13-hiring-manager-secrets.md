I spent six years in HR benefits administration at Nedbank. I processed enrolments, handled exits, answered hundreds of calls from staff who needed help navigating their benefits. I also sat in on enough hiring conversations to understand what actually moves a candidate forward — and what quietly kills an application before it is ever discussed.

Here is what hiring managers in South African offices are genuinely looking for when they fill an admin role.

## Accuracy over speed

Every admin posting mentions "attention to detail." What it actually means is this: they need someone who does not make mistakes that come back as problems later. In banking and HR administration, a single data entry error can affect payroll, benefits, or compliance records for dozens of people.

When you are in an interview, give concrete examples of how your work is checked, how you catch errors, and what your personal process looks like when you are handling large volumes of data. Do not just say you are "detail-oriented." Prove it.

## Composure under pressure

Office admin in South Africa — especially in banking, HR, and customer-facing roles — can be high-volume and emotionally demanding. A rude client. A system that is down. A deadline that moved without warning.

Hiring managers want to know you do not fall apart. Tell them about a specific time things went sideways and how you handled it. The story does not need to end perfectly. It needs to show that you stayed functional.

## Communication that suits the environment

Different workplaces have different communication cultures. A call centre rewards speed and a certain warmth. A compliance team values precise, formal language. A retail banking branch needs someone who can shift tone between a nervous first-time customer and a senior colleague asking for a report.

Tell the interviewer about the different communication environments you have worked in. It signals adaptability.

## Basic tech fluency — and the willingness to learn more

Most South African admin roles require Microsoft Word, Excel, and Outlook at minimum. Some now require SAP, Sage, or other HR systems. If you do not know a system they use, say so — and immediately follow it with what you did when you had to learn something new on the job.

Hiring managers are not looking for someone who knows everything. They are looking for someone who will not be a problem six weeks in.

## Reliability above everything

This is the one that does not appear in job postings but underlies every hiring decision: will this person show up, do the work, and not create drama?

References matter here. So does your tone in the interview. So does whether you arrive on time, whether you followed the application instructions, and whether you communicate clearly when you have a question.

Reliability is not exciting to talk about. But it is the single biggest factor in whether a candidate gets the offer.
