## What Is a Panel Interview?

A panel interview is conducted by two or more people simultaneously. Typically it includes:

- The hiring manager (who will be your direct supervisor)
- An HR representative
- One or two colleagues or senior stakeholders from the relevant department

Panel interviews are standard practice in banking, corporate administration, government, and most professional roles in South Africa. They are used because they reduce individual bias and get a broader view of a candidate in a single session.

## Why Candidates Struggle in Panel Interviews

The most common problem is not lack of knowledge — it is nerves. Being assessed by a group feels more formal and more high-stakes than a one-on-one conversation. Candidates freeze, give shorter answers, avoid eye contact, or direct all their attention to the person who asked the question and ignore the others.

Understanding the format removes most of the fear. A panel interview is still just a conversation with people who want to fill a vacancy. Your job is the same: demonstrate that you can do the work, that you will fit the team, and that you are reliable.

## Before the Interview

### Research Each Panelist

If you know who will be on the panel (HR often tells you in advance, or you can ask), search for them on LinkedIn. Understand their role, their background, and what they are likely to care about.

The HR panelist will focus on culture fit, communication, and general competency.
The line manager will focus on technical skills and practical experience.
A senior stakeholder will focus on judgment, professionalism, and strategic thinking.

Knowing who is in the room helps you frame your answers for multiple audiences.

### Prepare Your Key Stories

Panel interviews in South Africa — especially in banking and corporate environments — almost always use **competency-based (behavioural) questions**. These follow the pattern:

*"Tell me about a time when you..."*

Prepare five to six strong examples from your work history that can flex across different questions. Each story should follow the **STAR format:**

- **Situation:** Brief context — where, when, what was happening
- **Task:** What you specifically needed to do
- **Action:** What you did (this is the most important part — be specific)
- **Result:** What the outcome was

Common panel questions to prepare for:
- Tell me about yourself
- Describe a time you dealt with a difficult client or customer
- Give an example of when you had to work under pressure
- Tell us about a mistake you made and what you learned
- Where do you see yourself in three to five years?
- Why do you want to work for this organisation?

### Prepare Questions to Ask

At the end of a panel interview you will almost always be asked: *"Do you have any questions for us?"*

This is not a formality — it is another assessment point. Candidates who say "No, I think you've covered everything" signal a lack of genuine interest.

Prepare three or four thoughtful questions:
- *"What does success look like in the first 90 days in this role?"*
- *"How would you describe the team culture here?"*
- *"What are the biggest challenges someone in this position typically faces?"*
- *"What career development opportunities exist for someone in this role?"*

## During the Interview

### Make Eye Contact With the Whole Panel

When answering a question, begin by making eye contact with the person who asked it, but then deliberately shift your gaze to include the other panelists as you continue your answer. You are speaking to all of them.

This is the single most impactful adjustment you can make in a panel interview. It signals confidence, awareness, and respect for all the interviewers.

### Do Not Rush

Silence is not failure. When asked a difficult question, it is perfectly acceptable to say: *"That's a great question — give me just a moment to think."* Taking five seconds to compose a strong answer is far better than blurting out the first thing that comes to mind.

### Mirror the Formality of the Room

Read the energy of the panel and match it. If they are formal and professional, stay formal. If they are relaxed and conversational, you can be slightly more at ease — but never drop your professionalism entirely. You are still in an interview.

### Be Consistent

Everything you say should be consistent with your CV and your previous answers. Panelists sometimes ask variations of the same question to test for consistency and honesty. If your story changes, it raises red flags.

## After the Interview

Send a brief thank-you email within 24 hours — to HR, who can share it with the panel. Keep it short:

> *"Thank you for the opportunity to meet with your panel today. I came away even more excited about the role and the team. I look forward to hearing from you."*

This takes two minutes and very few candidates do it. It leaves a professional impression.

## The Bottom Line

Panel interviews are won in the preparation room, not the interview room. Know your stories. Know who you are speaking to. Make eye contact with everyone. Ask good questions. Follow up. The panel format is intimidating only until you understand it — then it becomes an opportunity to impress several decision-makers at once.
