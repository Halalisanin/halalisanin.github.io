## The 90-Second Rule

Psychologists and hiring researchers consistently find that interviewers form powerful first impressions within the first minute and a half of meeting a candidate. These impressions aren't always conscious, but they're real and they're sticky.

This doesn't mean superficiality rules. It means that confidence, presentation, and demeanour signal something important: *"This person takes this seriously. This person respects me and this organisation."*

Your skills and experience get you in the room. Your first impression determines whether the interviewer is rooting for you or looking for reasons to doubt you.

## Professional Dress for South African Interviews

When in doubt, overdress. It's far better to arrive slightly more formal than the office culture and have them think "sharp" than to arrive too casual and have them think "doesn't understand professional norms."

### Corporate and Banking Roles
**Men:** Suit jacket and trousers (matching or well-coordinated), button-up shirt, tie optional but professional, polished formal shoes, clean and neat.

**Women:** Formal suit (trouser or skirt), blouse or formal top, conservative dress, formal heels or flat formal shoes. Avoid heavy jewellery and very bright colours that distract.

### Office Administration and Professional Services
Business professional or smart business casual. Think: ironed, clean, fitted, conservative.

**Men:** Pressed chinos or formal trousers, collared shirt (tucked in), clean leather shoes.
**Women:** Formal blouse and tailored trousers, pencil skirt, or a professional dress.

### Practical Roles (Logistics, Retail Management, etc.)
Smart casual minimum. Even if the role involves being on your feet all day, the interview requires a step up from what the job itself involves.

### General Rules for All Interviews
- **Clothes must be ironed and clean.** This is non-negotiable. Creased clothing signals carelessness.
- **Shoes matter more than most candidates realise.** They're often the first thing experienced interviewers notice.
- **Avoid strong perfume or cologne.** You're in a closed room, less is more.
- **Nails should be neat.** If painted, choose a conservative colour.
- **Hair should be neat and professional.** Your natural hair, in any style, is absolutely fine. What matters is that it's tidy and intentional.
- **Visible tattoos and piercings:** In conservative industries like banking, it's advisable to cover visible tattoos and wear conservative jewellery. This is the reality of South Africa's corporate norms, it may change, but know your audience.

## Arrival: What Happens Before the Room

### Be Early
Aim to arrive 10 to 15 minutes before your appointment. This gives you time to find the building, settle your nerves, and compose yourself. Rushing in at the last second flustered is one of the worst possible starts.

Arriving more than 20 minutes early can be awkward for the reception staff, wait in your car or a nearby coffee shop if you're very early.

### Your Interaction With Reception
The way you treat the receptionist is sometimes reported back to the hiring manager. It sounds unfair, but it happens. Be warm, professional, and patient. Introduce yourself clearly: *"Good morning, I'm Sipho Dlamini, I've an interview appointment at 10 o'clock with Ms Mokoena."*

Switch your phone to silent **before** you enter the building.

## Body Language That Communicates Confidence

### Handshake
In South Africa's corporate environment, a firm (not crushing) handshake is still standard when meeting interviewers. Make eye contact, smile briefly, say your name.

If the interviewer doesn't offer their hand (for cultural or health reasons), a respectful nod and verbal greeting are perfectly appropriate.

### Posture
Sit up straight, not rigid, but upright and engaged. Leaning back in your chair reads as disinterested. Leaning too far forward can feel intrusive. Plant your feet flat on the floor.

### Eye Contact
Maintain natural, consistent eye contact. Look away occasionally, sustained unbroken eye contact reads as aggressive. In panel interviews, distribute your eye contact among all interviewers.

### Hands
Keep them visible and still. Fidgeting (pen clicking, ring twisting, hair touching) signals anxiety and is distracting. If you need to gesture to emphasise a point, that's fine, but keep gestures moderate.

### Facial Expression
Smile warmly when you greet and when it's natural. Show genuine engagement when they speak, nod, respond to what they say. A blank face during the interview can read as bored or anxious.

## Video and Remote Interviews

If your interview is conducted online, the same rules apply, adjusted for the format:

- **Dress fully** in professional attire (including your lower half, standing up by accident in pyjama trousers isn't a recoverable moment)
- **Test your technology** the day before: internet connection, camera, microphone, the video platform itself
- **Lighting:** Face a light source (window or lamp). Being backlit turns you into a silhouette
- **Background:** Plain, clean wall or a tidy room. A professional virtual background is acceptable if your environment is unavoidably messy
- **Eye contact on video:** Look into the camera, not at your own image on screen. This is the equivalent of eye contact
- **Reduce interruptions:** Tell household members, put pets in another room, turn off notifications

## The Lasting Impression

Every interview ends with a handshake (or a warm farewell on video) and a thank-you. Be gracious and genuine. Whatever happened in the interview, whether you think it went well or not, leave on a professional, positive note.

*"Thank you so much for your time. I've genuinely enjoyed learning more about the role and the team. I look forward to hearing from you."*

That final impression walks out of the room with the interviewer after you leave. Make it a good one.
